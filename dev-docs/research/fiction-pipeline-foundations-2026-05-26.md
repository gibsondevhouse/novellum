# Fiction Craft, Prior Art, and Prompt-Schema Research for Novellum

## 1. The Anatomy of a Novel

A novel works because it operates at several structural layers at once. The **work** is the whole creative object a reader can point to: sometimes one book, sometimes an entire series or world. The **book** is one complete reading experience with its own beginning, middle, and end. A **part** is an optional big divider that groups chapters into larger movements. An **act** is a major shift in the storyÔÇÖs direction: setup, escalation, resolution in a simple three-act model. A **chapter** is a reader-facing unit of delivery: something small enough to read in one sitting, but large enough to carry momentum. A **scene** is where something happens in continuous time and space. A **beat** is the smallest meaningful unit of change inside a scene: a glance, a revelation, a decision, a blow, a lie, a confession. Story Grid explicitly treats beats, scenes, sequences, quadrants, and the whole story as nested ÔÇťunits of story,ÔÇŁ while three-act structure and Save the Cat operate more strongly at the act-and-turning-point level. ţłÇciteţłéturn15view0ţłéturn15view1ţłéturn29search0ţłéturn8view5ţłü

For a non-writer, the simplest way to think about these layers is this. The **book** answers the big dramatic question. The **acts** organise the big movement of pressure. The **chapters** package that movement into readable chunks. The **scenes** force characters to do things under pressure. The **beats** make those scenes feel alive instead of summarized. If your pipeline tries to skip straight from ÔÇťbook ideaÔÇŁ to ÔÇťnovel prose,ÔÇŁ it skips the very layers where most coherence is created. ### Three-act structure

Three-act structure prescribes a simple macro-spine: **Act I** establishes the world and launches the problem, **Act II** escalates pressure and complicates the protagonistÔÇÖs attempts to solve it, and **Act III** resolves the central dramatic question. It suits writers who need a strong high-level scaffold without a lot of doctrine. The main artifact it produces is an **act map** with two major turning points and a climax. In Novellum terms, it maps most naturally onto **arc Ôćĺ act Ôćĺ chapter/scene**. The acts table is the most obvious persistence target for this framework, while the turning points and climactic decisions naturally cascade down into chapters, scenes, and beats. ţłÇciteţłéturn29search0ţłéturn29search3ţłü

### Save the Cat

Save the Cat gives a more specific rhythm than three-act structure. Its core promise is a **15-beat roadmap** from Opening Image to Final Image, designed to keep pacing and character transformation aligned. It suits beginners, commercially minded writers, and anyone who wants explicit ÔÇťwhat should happen around here?ÔÇŁ guidance. The artifact it produces is a **beat sheet** and often a card-based beat board. In NovellumÔÇÖs hierarchy, it maps cleanly as a **global arc skeleton** first, then as **act-level turning points**, then as **chapter- and scene-level assignments** for where the beats are actually paid off. Its biggest strength is clarity; its biggest weakness is that users often mistake it for a fill-in-the-blanks formula rather than a pacing tool. ţłÇciteţłéturn8view5ţłéturn43view1ţłü

### Story Grid

Story Grid is less ÔÇťwhat happens on page 12?ÔÇŁ and more ÔÇťwhat kind of story are you promising, and does every unit pay that promise off?ÔÇŁ It prescribes genre identification, conventions, obligatory moments, a controlling idea, and the Five Commandments of Storytelling: **inciting incident, progressive complication, crisis, climax, resolution**. K.M. WeilandÔÇÖs approach is explicitly writer-friendly and anti-outline-anxiety. ### Snowflake method

The Snowflake method is an iterative expansion system. It begins with a one-sentence summary, expands to a paragraph, then character sheets, then longer synopses, then a scene spreadsheet. SandersonÔÇÖs public teaching is less a single branded structure and more a toolkit: plotting versus discovery, viewpoint discipline, scene purpose, character stakes, and especially in fantasy, the importance of understandable systems and constraints. ## 5. The Story Outline, as a Schema

A usable outline is hierarchical and relational. It must let the author zoom from big promise to line-of-action without losing continuity. Snowflake is useful here because it expands from one sentence to a paragraph to character documents to scene lists; Story Grid is useful because it asks whether each unit actually turns; K.M. Weiland is useful because she explicitly treats scene organization as the final outlining step before drafting. ţłÇciteţłéturn32view0ţłéturn15view0ţłéturn31view0ţłü

### Arc

An arc record should contain: **title**, **dramatic question**, **protagonist or focus group**, **antagonistic force**, **start state**, **end state**, **purpose**, **status**, **linked themes**, and **order**. Example from *Mistborn*: arc title ÔÇťBreak the Final Empire,ÔÇŁ dramatic question ÔÇťCan a rebellion built by thieves topple a god-emperor?ÔÇŁ The current `arcs` table maps well to title, description, purpose, type, status, and order, but a stronger dramatic-question field would improve review-stage precision.

An act record should contain: **title**, **act function**, **entry condition**, **main escalation**, **turning point**, **act-end disaster or commitment**, and **linked arc ids**. ### Beat

A beat record should contain: **title**, **type**, **moment summary**, **who acts**, **what changes**, **emotional effect**, **linked scene id**, and possibly **linked arc id** for macro beats. ### Cross-references to the world bible

A strong outline must refer outward. Scene Ôćĺ POV character record. Scene Ôćĺ location record. Scene Ôćĺ timeline event. Scene Ôćĺ thread/subplot. Chapter Ôćĺ arc refs. Arc Ôćĺ theme. ## 7. Prompt Engineering for Long-Form Fiction

One-shot ÔÇťwrite me a novelÔÇŁ prompting fails for reasons the research now makes very plain. Long-form fiction needs global coherence, premise relevance, temporal consistency, stable character facts, and escalating causality across many thousands of words. Planning papers repeatedly show that explicit pre-generation planning improves coherence and relevance over direct generation, and newer long-story work shows that revision loops outperform single-pass drafting. At the same time, recent consistency-benchmark work shows that long-form LLM stories still break factual, temporal, and character continuity in predictable ways. StoryScopeÔÇÖs 2026 analysis adds an important qualitative warning: AI fiction tends to over-explain themes and collapse toward tidy single-track plots, which is exactly what a ÔÇťwrite me a novelÔÇŁ command encourages. ţłÇciteţłéturn34academia0ţłéturn34academia1ţłéturn35academia0ţłéturn40academia1ţłü

Context management matters more than raw context-window size. ÔÇťLost in the MiddleÔÇŁ showed that long-context models do not use all context equally well; relevant information buried in the middle is often handled worse than information at the start or end. For fiction design, that means the pipeline should not naively dump the whole bible and outline into every call. The better pattern is **scene-plus-adjacent context**: load the approved scene brief, the immediately previous and next outline states, the POV character record, the location record, the linked lore rules, and the specific threads advanced. For larger memory, use retrieval, not blob prompting. RAG work supports the general idea of retrieving only task-relevant external memory; FACT and retrieval-structuring work suggest iterative retrieval and structured intermediate knowledge are more reliable than one-pass ÔÇťstuff everything in.ÔÇŁ ţłÇciteţłéturn36academia2ţłéturn36academia0ţłéturn35academia1ţłéturn36academia3ţłü

A good long-form fiction memory ladder usually has four layers. The **global ladder** is the one-page book plan: premise, theme, tone, genre promise, and ending polarity. The **middle ladder** is arc and act summaries. The **local ladder** is chapter and scene briefs. The **entity ladder** is the world bible: characters, places, lore rules, timeline, threads. Re3 and RecurrentGPT both support the general principle that long text generation becomes more stable when the model is repeatedly re-prompted with refreshed state rather than asked to carry all memory implicitly. ţłÇciteţłéturn34academia1ţłéturn39academia3ţłü

Structured output should dominate the planning stages. Premise, Worldspec, Research, World Bible population, Book Premise, Outline, and Revision Pack should all prefer **schema-validated JSON** or another similarly strict structured format. The one major exception is **Scene Drafts**, where the primary output must be prose. Even there, the model should emit prose with a required metadata sidecar. The reason is simple: planning artifacts are easier to validate, review, diff, store, and selectively accept when structured. But there is a caveat from schema-generation research: LLMs still fail valid JSON generation often enough that schemas should stay modest, validators should be strict, and stage outputs should be broken into smaller objects rather than one huge mega-schema. ţłÇciteţłéturn38academia1ţłéturn38academia0ţłéturn38academia2ţłü

For NovellumÔÇÖs pipeline, a sensible split is this. **JSON-only** for premise, worldspec, research brief, world-bible records, high-level outline, and revision pack. **Prose-plus-metadata** for scene drafts. **Optional prose-plus-JSON** for outline stages where you want both a human-readable synopsis and machine-usable scene cards. That arrangement fits the research and matches the product constraint that the human reviews and accepts stage outputs before they become canon. ţłÇciteţłéturn34academia0ţłéturn34academia1ţłéturn38academia1ţłü

Voice and style consistency need lighter, repeatable conditioning rather than huge overwrite prompts. Research on prompt engineering shows that role-based and example-based prompting can be helpful, but persona and style conditioning are sensitive to formulation, and style control remains brittle. Recent work on style-conditioned long-form story generation suggests that style can be nudged, but completeness and coherence remain separate problems. The practical lesson is to use a **style card** rather than ÔÇťsound like X.ÔÇŁ A good style card names sentence-length tendencies, dialogue density, descriptive density, narrative distance, taboo words, and one or two short exemplar passages owned by the user. Avoid ÔÇťwrite like a living authorÔÇŁ prompts; use **self-style** or **project-style** anchors instead. ţłÇciteţłéturn37academia0ţłéturn37academia3ţłéturn39academia1ţłéturn38academia0ţłü

Continuity preservation should be explicit, not aspirational. The research says narrative consistency bugs cluster especially in factual and temporal dimensions. So the pipeline should treat continuity as a separate loop: extract facts after each accepted stage, store them structurally, retrieve only relevant facts for the next generation call, and run contradiction checks before approval. That complements NovellumÔÇÖs existing `ContinuityAgent` well. In practice, this means entity tracking, timeline enforcement, canonical fact extraction, contradiction detection, and revision-pack surfacing. Re3ÔÇÖs revision step, FACTÔÇÖs multi-fact retrieval findings, and newer consistency-bench work all point in the same direction: long-form fiction is not mainly a ÔÇťbetter promptÔÇŁ problem; it is a **memory-and-verification** problem. ţłÇciteţłéturn35academia0ţłéturn34academia1ţłéturn35academia1ţłü

## 8. Recommended Stage-by-Stage Prompt Outlines

### Vibe-Worldbuild ÔÇö Premise

**ROLE:** Fiction-development assistant helping define a book-worthy story premise.

**TASK:** Turn the userÔÇÖs raw idea into a compact, reviewable premise card.

**CONTEXT:** Load current `projects` title/genre/logline/synopsis if present, `story_frames`, and any user-approved notes in `project_metadata` **[uncertain]**. If an earlier premise exists, load it as reference, not as fixed canon.

**CONSTRAINTS:** Do not invent a whole cast. Do not define detailed magic or political systems yet. Do not turn tone words into plot claims unless the user endorsed them. Do not pretend unresolved questions are settled canon.

**OUTPUT FORMAT:** Small JSON object with fields such as `hook`, `genreBlend`, `readerPromise`, `coreConflict`, `worldPressure`, `tone`, `scope`, `nonNegotiables`, `openQuestions`.

### Vibe-Worldbuild ÔÇö Worldspec

**ROLE:** World design assistant translating premise into constraints.

**TASK:** Produce a worldspec that explains how this world works and what kinds of conflict it naturally generates.

**CONTEXT:** Load approved premise from `story_frames` and prior accepted world notes from `lore_entries`; optionally load seed `locations` and `characters` if the author already created any.

**CONSTRAINTS:** Do not create encyclopedic lore. Do not create rules without costs or limitations. Do not introduce named factions as if they are canon unless the user approves them. Keep focus on systems that affect plots and choices.

**OUTPUT FORMAT:** JSON object with `realityMode`, `environment`, `powerOrder`, `socialOrder`, `scarcity`, `magicOrTechRules`, `taboos`, `ordinaryLifeBaseline`, `conflictEngines`, `aestheticAnchors`, `questionsForResearch`.

### Vibe-Worldbuild ÔÇö Research

**ROLE:** Story research organizer, not silent canon maker.

**TASK:** Turn unresolved worldspec questions into decision-oriented research briefs.

**CONTEXT:** Load approved worldspec, unresolved questions, related `lore_entries`, and relevant `timeline_events`. If source-note persistence exists in `project_metadata` **[uncertain]**, load prior research notes there too.

**CONSTRAINTS:** Clearly separate sourced findings from invented canon. Do not canonize anything without an explicit `recommendation` field. Do not answer every curiosity; answer the questions that affect story logic.

**OUTPUT FORMAT:** JSON array `researchBriefs[]` with fields like `question`, `whyItMatters`, `candidateAnswers`, `selectedRecommendation`, `confidence`, `sourceNote`, `canonImpact`.

### Vibe-Worldbuild ÔÇö Populated World Bible

**ROLE:** Canon-assembly assistant building reusable story entities.

**TASK:** Convert approved premise, worldspec, and research into structured world-bible records.

**CONTEXT:** Load `story_frames`, approved `lore_entries`, `timeline_events`, and any existing `characters`, `character_relationships`, `locations`, and `plot_threads`.

**CONSTRAINTS:** Do not create entities with no story role. Do not duplicate near-identical entries. Do not create factions as vague labels; if faction modeling is weak in-schema, mark them clearly for review. Do not hide unresolved contradictions.

**OUTPUT FORMAT:** JSON object containing arrays such as `characters[]`, `locations[]`, `factions[]`, `loreEntries[]`, `timelineEvents[]`, `themes[]`, `glossary[]`, and `relationships[]`, with entity references where possible. Where a direct persistence home is weak, mark `needsSchemaExtension: true`.

### Vibe-Author ÔÇö Premise

**ROLE:** Novel-planning assistant narrowing the story to one book.

**TASK:** Turn the approved world setup into a book-shaped premise with a concrete protagonist problem.

**CONTEXT:** Load `story_frames`, accepted world-bible tables (`characters`, `locations`, `lore_entries`, `timeline_events`, `plot_threads`, `character_relationships`), and existing `projects` summary fields.

**CONSTRAINTS:** Do not try to tell the entire series. Do not invent world rules not present in the bible. Do not choose a protagonist or antagonist force unless the output states why this choice produces the strongest book.

**OUTPUT FORMAT:** JSON with `protagonistId` **[or candidate if not yet canonical]**, `externalGoal`, `internalNeed`, `antagonisticForce`, `stakes`, `dramaticQuestion`, `themeHypothesis`, `endingPolarity`, `scopeStatement`.

### Vibe-Author ÔÇö Outline

**ROLE:** Structural planning assistant.

**TASK:** Produce a causally escalating outline from arc down to beat, with entity cross-references.

**CONTEXT:** Load accepted author premise, all approved world-bible records, existing `arcs`, `acts`, `chapters`, `scenes`, `beats`, `plot_threads`, and `timeline_events`.

**CONSTRAINTS:** Every scene must change something. Do not create scene cards without POV, location, and thread references. Do not jump ahead to prose. Do not invent characters or locations not present in the bible unless flagged as candidate additions for review.

**OUTPUT FORMAT:** JSON object with nested `arcs[]`, `acts[]`, `chapters[]`, `scenes[]`, `beats[]`. Each `scene` should include `povCharacterId`, `locationId`, `timelineEventId` if applicable, `threadIds` **[needs direct field support if absent]**, `goal`, `conflict`, `turn`, `outcome`, `arcRefs`.

### Vibe-Author ÔÇö Scene Drafts

**ROLE:** Scene drafter operating under tight canon and outline constraints.

**TASK:** Draft one approved scene in prose while emitting machine-usable metadata.

**CONTEXT:** Load the approved scene row from `scenes`, linked `beats`, adjacent scene summaries, POV character from `characters`, location from `locations`, linked `lore_entries`, relevant `plot_threads`, current `consistency_issues`, and any user-approved style cues from `writing_styles`, `system_prompts`, or `chat_instructions`.

**CONSTRAINTS:** Do not invent major canon. Do not advance the plot beyond the assigned scene turn. Stay in the assigned POV and time. Respect established world rules. If a needed fact is missing, surface it as uncertainty instead of improvising canon.

**OUTPUT FORMAT:** Prose contract plus JSON sidecar. The sidecar should require `sceneId`, `assignedBeatIds`, `povCharacterId`, `locationId`, `characterIds`, `threadIds` **[uncertain direct persistence]**, `factsIntroduced`, `continuityRisks`, `wordCountEstimate`, `draftStatus`.

### Vibe-Author ÔÇö Revision Pack

**ROLE:** Editorial analyst, not silent rewriter.

**TASK:** Diagnose a scene or chapter draft and propose finite, reviewable revisions.

**CONTEXT:** Load current prose from `scenes.content`, latest `scene_snapshots`, approved outline nodes, related world-bible records, and current `consistency_issues`.

**CONSTRAINTS:** Do not overwrite manuscript text. Diagnose before prescribing. Rank issues by severity and impact. Distinguish continuity fixes from stylistic preferences. Do not recommend changes that violate approved canon.

**OUTPUT FORMAT:** JSON object with `issues[]` where each issue has `severity`, `type`, `evidenceSpan`, `problemExplanation`, `minimalFix`, `deeperRewriteOption`, `requiresCanonDecision`, `relatedEntityIds`, and `approvalChecklist`.

## 9. The Teaching Layer

### Vibe-Worldbuild ÔÇö Premise

**Craft concepts:** story promise; pressure.  
**Minimum explanation:** A premise is not ÔÇťeverything about your book.ÔÇŁ It is the promise you make to the reader about what kind of trouble they are signing up for. Good premises usually combine a person under pressure with a world that makes that pressure worse. If the pressure disappeared, the story would become a different book.  
**Examples to show:** *Mistborn* for ÔÇťrebellion in an oppressive magical empireÔÇŁ; *Dune* for ÔÇťpolitics and survival inside a hostile resource world.ÔÇŁ ţłÇciteţłéturn28search0ţłéturn27search4ţłü  
**Anti-pattern:** ÔÇťA young woman discovers secrets in a magical worldÔÇŁ ÔÇö too vague to guide anything.  
**Check question:** What is the one thing about this world that makes your story impossible to mistake for a different one?

### Vibe-Worldbuild ÔÇö Worldspec

**Craft concepts:** constraints; story engine.  
**Minimum explanation:** A world becomes interesting when it creates problems, not when it collects facts. Ask what is scarce, dangerous, forbidden, expensive, shameful, or politically explosive. Those are the pressure points that later create scenes. The goal is not maximal detail; it is the right detail.  
**Examples to show:** *Dune* for water and spice; *Earthsea* for true names and balance; *Discworld* for institutions used as comic engines. ţłÇciteţłéturn27search4ţłéturn28search3ţłéturn28search2ţłü  
**Anti-pattern:** endless lore that never changes a characterÔÇÖs options.  
**Check question:** What two world rules will regularly force your characters into hard decisions?

### Vibe-Worldbuild ÔÇö Research

**Craft concepts:** selective research; decision over collection.  
**Minimum explanation:** Research is not homework for its own sake. It exists to answer the questions that would otherwise make the world feel fake, thin, or confused. Good research ends in a decision: ÔÇťIn this book, it works like this.ÔÇŁ Bad research ends in a pile of notes.  
**Examples to show:** desert survival for *Dune*-like settings; seafaring and island logic for *Earthsea*-like settings. ţłÇciteţłéturn27search4ţłéturn28search3ţłü  
**Anti-pattern:** spending hours reading and still not knowing what your canon answer is.  
**Check question:** What story decision will this research change?

### Vibe-Worldbuild ÔÇö Populated World Bible

**Craft concepts:** relational canon; story relevance.  
**Minimum explanation:** A world bible is most useful when its entries connect to each other. Characters belong somewhere, want something, fear something, and affect other people. Places matter because events happen there. Lore matters because it enables or limits action. If an entry has no story job, it probably does not need to be first-class canon yet.

**Anti-pattern:** detailed character sheets with no goals, no contradictions, and no links to the plot.  
**Check question:** Can you say how this entry affects at least one other entry?

### Vibe-Author ÔÇö Premise

**Craft concepts:** protagonist; stakes.  
**Minimum explanation:** A world does not make a novel by itself. A novel needs someone who wants something concrete and must pay a price to get it. Stakes answer the question ÔÇťwhy canÔÇÖt they just walk away?ÔÇŁ If failure changes nothing, the premise still is not sharp enough.  
**Examples to show:** Paul in *Dune*; Ged in *A Wizard of Earthsea*. ţłÇciteţłéturn27search4ţłéturn28search3ţłü  
**Anti-pattern:** trying to make the ÔÇťpremiseÔÇŁ cover every subplot and every book in a future series.  
**Check question:** If your protagonist fails, what specifically gets worse?

### Vibe-Author ÔÇö Outline

**Craft concepts:** causality; escalation.  
**Minimum explanation:** An outline is a chain of ÔÇťbecause of that, this happens next,ÔÇŁ not ÔÇťand then this happens.ÔÇŁ Each act should remove options and raise pressure. Each scene should either change the situation, reveal something that changes the situation, or force a commitment. That is what prevents a mushy middle.  
**Examples to show:** the rebellion mechanics of *Mistborn*; the betrayal-survival-rise shape of *Dune*. ţłÇciteţłéturn28search0ţłéturn27search4ţłü  
**Anti-pattern:** chapters that are individually pleasant but removable.  
**Check question:** What changes because this scene happens?

### Vibe-Author ÔÇö Scene drafts

**Craft concepts:** scene objective; turn.  
**Minimum explanation:** A scene begins with a want and becomes interesting when something blocks or bends that want. By the end of the scene, some meaningful value should have changed: trust to suspicion, safety to danger, ignorance to knowledge, hope to dread. Description and dialogue should serve that turn rather than float around it.  
**Examples to show:** PaulÔÇÖs pain-box test in *Dune*; GedÔÇÖs rivalry and disastrous overreach in *Earthsea*. ţłÇciteţłéturn27search4ţłéturn28search3ţłü  
**Anti-pattern:** exposition scenes where nobody is trying to do anything now.  
**Check question:** What is different at the end of the scene than at the beginning?

### Vibe-Author ÔÇö Revision pack

**Craft concepts:** diagnosis before polish; reader effect.  
**Minimum explanation:** Revision starts with purpose, not sentence polish. First ask whether the scene does the job it was meant to do. Then ask whether it is clear, tense, and consistent with canon. Only after that should you worry about line-level elegance.  
**Examples to show:** Story GridÔÇÖs Five Commandments are useful here because they ask whether the scene has incident, complication, crisis, climax, and resolution. ţłÇciteţłéturn15view0ţłü  
**Anti-pattern:** rewriting lines beautifully in a scene whose core turn is still weak.  
**Check question:** What is the one most important reaction this scene should produce in the reader?

## 10. Risks, Pitfalls, and Open Questions

Hallucination and continuity drift are not edge cases in long-form fiction generation; they are normal failure modes. The current research says consistency errors in long stories are especially strong in factual and temporal dimensions, and long-context models still handle buried information weakly. That means the pipeline should assume memory drift and build against it with explicit retrieval, structured canon, contradiction checks, and review packs rather than hoping ÔÇťthe model remembers.ÔÇŁ ţłÇciteţłéturn35academia0ţłéturn36academia2ţłéturn34academia1ţłü

User overwhelm versus user underwhelm is a real design tension. Too many checkpoints make the process feel like bureaucracy; too few let the AI run ahead and collapse authorship. Human-AI co-writing research suggests scaffolding level matters: too little help can fail to improve outcomes, while high scaffolding can help especially less experienced users, but may reduce ownership and satisfaction. The right answer is probably not one fixed number of gates, but a few **high-value gates** at points where canon hardens: premise, worldspec, book premise, outline, and post-draft revision. ţłÇciteţłéturn40academia4ţłéturn40academia0ţłü

IP and copyright deserve a conservative stance. I did **not** independently verify the latest May 2026 legal guidance in this pass, so this is design prudence rather than legal advice. The safest product posture is to avoid ÔÇťimitate living authorÔÇŁ workflows, preserve records of user decisions and edits, separate generated suggestions from accepted canon, and maintain provenance of what the human approved or rewrote. If Novellum later needs policy-level certainty on publication, contest eligibility, or copyright registration posture, that should be treated as a separate legal research task.

The ÔÇťis this still my book?ÔÇŁ question is not hypothetical. Research on scaffolded co-writing found a moderate decrease in text ownership and satisfaction under higher-scaffolding conditions, even when output quality improved. That is exactly why the review gate matters. If the tool silently mutates manuscript prose, ownership will feel lower. If the tool proposes, structures, and diagnoses but leaves acceptance explicit, authorship is psychologically easier to defend. ţłÇciteţłéturn40academia4ţłéturn40academia0ţłü

Cost runaway is a practical risk even before legal or artistic ones. I did **not** verify current OpenRouter model prices in this pass, so the numbers below are planning math, not live quotes. A 90,000-word novel is roughly on the order of 110,000ÔÇô130,000 output tokens for the final prose alone. A staged pipeline with worldbuilding, outline, scene drafting, continuity checks, and revision packs can easily multiply that several times over, especially if users regenerate often. As a rough budgeting frame: a disciplined low-cost pipeline will usually land in the **tens of dollars**; repeated use of mid-tier premium models can push the same project into the **low hundreds**; frontier-model-heavy retries across every stage can climb into the **several hundreds**. The cost control levers are stage-gating, smaller schemas, scene-level context instead of whole-manuscript prompting, and banning casual rerolls.

The teaching layer can also backfire. Non-writers need help, but writers hate being condescended to. The solution is not to remove teaching; it is to make it **optional, stage-specific, and competence-sensitive**. Teach the one concept the user needs now, in plain language, then get out of the way. Users who already know the craft should be able to collapse the explanations and proceed with the structured decision itself. ţłÇciteţłéturn40academia4ţłü

The five most important open questions before building are these:

1. **What exactly becomes canon at each gate?** If the answer is fuzzy, the AI will either over-assume or under-help.  
2. **How much schema strictness is acceptable before the product feels like paperwork?** This is the central product constraint, not a minor detail.  
3. **Where will factions, themes, glossary terms, and research provenance live if they become first-class?** The current schema is strong in some areas and thin in others.  
4. **What is the default cost tier and regeneration policy?** Without this, ÔÇťgreat UXÔÇŁ can quietly become unaffordable UX.  
5. **How much teaching is default versus on-demand?** The answer determines whether the product feels like a mentor, a tool, or a nag.
